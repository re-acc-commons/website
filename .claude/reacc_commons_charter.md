## Decision-Making

### Three Governing Principles

**Sociocratic Consent.** Decisions move forward unless someone raises a paramount objection â€” a concern that the proposal would harm the commons' ability to function toward its shared aims. Silence after the consent window closes is not blocking.

**Subsidiarity.** Decisions are made at the most local level capable of handling them. Individual choices don't require group consent. Foundational changes do.

**Reversibility Determines Speed.** Easy-to-reverse changes move quickly. Hard-to-reverse changes require more deliberation. The cost of being wrong determines the weight of the process.

### Paramount Objections

Not every disagreement is a paramount objection. To qualify, an objection must articulate:

1. **The Harm.** How does this proposal block our ability to function toward our shared aims?
2. **The Path Forward.** Either integration criteria (what would need to change for you to consent?) OR a counter-proposal.

**Template:**
> "I object to [proposal] because [specific harm to collective function]. I consent if [integration criteria], OR I propose [alternative]."

This filters out:
- Personal preference ("I don't like this approach") â†’ not paramount
- Aesthetic disagreement ("This isn't how I'd frame it") â†’ not paramount
- Systemic risk ("This creates dynamics that undermine trust") â†’ valid

**Resolution.** Objections are integrated, not overruled. The goal is a proposal everyone can align with, not forced compliance.

### Call-Up Mechanism

## Accountability & Enforcement

The membranes must be maintained or the commons dissolves. This is immune function, not punishment. But it has teeth.

### Escalating Response Framework

**Stage 1: Peer Dialogue.** Direct conversation. A fellow participant shares their observation and concern. This is solidarity â€” mutual support in living our values. Most situations resolve here.

**Stage 2: Circle Reflection.** If unresolved, the relevant working circle holds a facilitated reflection. The participant shares their perspective. The circle names the pattern and the principles in tension. Together they seek a path forward. Documented in `#accountability` with appropriate privacy.

**Stage 3: Stewardship Mediation.** If the pattern persists, Stewards facilitate mediation. Clear agreements are made with specific, observable commitments and a defined review period.

**Stage 4: Commons Review.** For sustained or severe misalignment â€” actions that harm the commons, violate trust, or represent fundamental departure from principles. The Stewardship convenes a review. Outcomes may include:
- Temporary suspension (defined period, clear re-entry criteria)
- Role reduction (return to a lower membrane with clear pathway back)
- Removal from commons

### Bot Enforcement

Bot-Enforcers execute moderation actions **only** after a documented accountability process has reached a clear outcome:

- **Automated**: Spam removal, obvious bad-faith actors (no prior engagement, clear disruption). Steward can reverse within 24h.
- **Stage 4 outcomes**: Bot executes role changes or removal only after Stewardship documents the decision and the consent process that produced it. Documentation must include: the concern, the process followed, the outcome, and the consent record.
- **No autonomous judgment.** Bots do not assess alignment. Humans assess alignment. Bots execute decisions humans have made through legitimate process.

### Emergency Removal

For actions that pose immediate harm to participants or the commons (doxxing, harassment, treasury theft):
- Any Steward can initiate immediate temporary suspension
- Post-hoc ratification by Stewardship within 48h
- Full accountability process follows
- Documented with emergency rationale

## Stewardship

### Rotating Curator Model

One Steward holds primary facilitation responsibility monthly. The Steward:

- Ensures consent processes are followed
- Merges governance PRs after consent is documented
- Facilitates accountability processes when triggered
- Calls for escalation when consent is ambiguous
- Maintains the `#stewardship` channel with transparent documentation

The Steward does **not** have:
- Tiebreaker power
- Veto authority
- Final say on content or direction
- Greater weight in consent processes

**Curator, not gatekeeper.** The Steward tends conditions for the commons to self-govern. The moment stewardship becomes governance, it has failed.

### Steward Selection

Any Member may be nominated (including self-nomination). Selection follows full commons consent: all Members, 72-hour objection window, no unresolved paramount objections. Monthly rotation ensures no one accumulates positional power.
